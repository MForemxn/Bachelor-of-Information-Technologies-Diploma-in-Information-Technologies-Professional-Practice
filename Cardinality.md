In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **cardinality** of a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") is a measure of the number of [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Element_(mathematics) "Element (mathematics)") of the set. For example, the set �={2,4,6}![{\displaystyle A=\{2,4,6\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/babb2cdea38eac589f1b05a633d2c94951bbb6bd) contains 3 elements, and therefore �![{\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) has a cardinality of 3. Beginning in the late 19th century, this concept was generalized to [infinite sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_set "Infinite set"), which allows one to distinguish between different types of infinity, and to perform [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_arithmetic "Cardinal arithmetic") on them. There are two approaches to cardinality: one which compares sets directly using [bijections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") and [injections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective_function "Injective function"), and another which uses [cardinal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-1) The cardinality of a set may also be called its **size**, when no confusion with other notions of size[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-2) is possible.

## History[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cardinality&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

A crude sense of cardinality, an awareness that groups of things or events compare with other groups by containing more, fewer, or the same number of instances, is observed in a variety of present-day animal species, suggesting an origin millions of years ago.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-4) Human expression of cardinality is seen as early as 40000 years ago, with equating the size of a group with a group of recorded notches, or a representative collection of other things, such as sticks and shells.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-5) The abstraction of cardinality as a number is evident by 3000 BCE, in Sumerian [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics "History of mathematics") and the manipulation of numbers without reference to a specific group of things or events.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-6)

From the 6th century BCE, the writings of Greek philosophers show hints of the cardinality of infinite sets. While they considered the notion of infinity as an endless series of actions, such as adding 1 to a number repeatedly, they did not consider the size of an infinite set of numbers to be a thing.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-Allen-7) The ancient Greek notion of infinity also considered the division of things into parts repeated without limit. In Euclid's _[Elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements")_, [commensurability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(mathematics) "Commensurability (mathematics)") was described as the ability to compare the length of two line segments, _a_ and _b_, as a ratio, as long as there were a third segment, no matter how small, that could be laid end-to-end a whole number of times into both _a_ and _b_. But with the discovery of [irrational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_number "Irrational number"), it was seen that even the infinite set of all rational numbers was not enough to describe the length of every possible line segment.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-8) Still, there was no concept of infinite sets as something that had cardinality.

To better understand infinite sets, a notion of cardinality was formulated circa 1880 by [Georg Cantor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Cantor "Georg Cantor"), the originator of [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"). He examined the process of equating two sets with [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection"), a one-to-one correspondence between the elements of two sets based on a unique relationship. In 1891, with the publication of [Cantor's diagonal argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor%27s_diagonal_argument "Cantor's diagonal argument"), he demonstrated that there are sets of numbers that cannot be placed in one-to-one correspondence with the set of natural numbers, i.e. [uncountable sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncountable_set "Uncountable set") that contain more elements than there are in the infinite set of natural numbers.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-9)

## Comparing sets[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cardinality&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Comparing sets")]

Bijective function from **N** to the set _E_ of [even numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_number "Even number"). Although _E_ is a proper subset of **N**, both sets have the same cardinality.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Diagonal_argument_powerset_svg.svg/250px-Diagonal_argument_powerset_svg.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Diagonal_argument_powerset_svg.svg)

**N** does not have the same cardinality as its [power set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_set "Power set") _P_(**N**): For every function _f_ from **N** to _P_(**N**), the set _T_ = {_n_∈**N**: _n_∉_f_(_n_)} disagrees with every set in the [range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Range_of_a_function "Range of a function") of _f_, hence _f_ cannot be surjective. The picture shows an example _f_ and the corresponding _T_; **red**: _n_∈_f_(_n_)\_T_, **blue**:_n_∈_T_\_f_(_n_).

While the cardinality of a finite set is just the number of its elements, extending the notion to infinite sets usually starts with defining the notion of comparison of arbitrary sets (some of which are possibly infinite).

### Definition 1: |_A_| = |_B_|[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cardinality&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Definition 1: |A| = |B|")]

Two sets _A_ and _B_ have the same cardinality if there exists a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") (a.k.a., one-to-one correspondence) from _A_ to _B_,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-:1-10) that is, a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") from _A_ to _B_ that is both [injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective "Injective") and [surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective "Surjective"). Such sets are said to be _equipotent_, _equipollent_, or _[equinumerous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equinumerous "Equinumerous")_. This relationship can also be denoted _A_ ≈ _B_ or _A_ ~ _B_.

For example, the set _E_ = {0, 2, 4, 6, ...} of non-negative [even numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_number "Even number") has the same cardinality as the set **N** = {0, 1, 2, 3, ...} of [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_numbers "Natural numbers"), since the function _f_(_n_) = 2_n_ is a bijection from **N** to _E_ (see picture).

For finite sets _A_ and _B_, if _some_ bijection exists from _A_ to _B_, then _each_ injective or surjective function from _A_ to _B_ is a bijection. This is no longer true for infinite _A_ and _B_. For example, the function _g_ from **N** to _E_, defined by _g_(_n_) = 4_n_ is injective, but not surjective, and _h_ from **N** to _E_, defined by _h_(_n_) = _n_ - (_n_ [mod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modulo_operation "Modulo operation") 2) is surjective, but not injective. Neither _g_ nor _h_ can challenge |_E_| = |**N**|, which was established by the existence of _f_.

### Definition 2: |_A_| ≤ |_B_|[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cardinality&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Definition 2: |A| ≤ |B|")]

_A_ has cardinality less than or equal to the cardinality of _B_, if there exists an injective function from _A_ into _B_.

### Definition 3: |_A_| < |_B_|[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cardinality&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Definition 3: |A| < |B|")]

_A_ has cardinality strictly less than the cardinality of _B_, if there is an injective function, but no bijective function, from _A_ to _B_.

For example, the set **N** of all [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_numbers "Natural numbers") has cardinality strictly less than its [power set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_set "Power set") _P_(**N**), because _g_(_n_) = { _n_ } is an injective function from **N** to _P_(**N**), and it can be shown that no function from **N** to _P_(**N**) can be bijective (see picture). By a similar argument, **N** has cardinality strictly less than the cardinality of the set **R** of all [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"). For proofs, see [Cantor's diagonal argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor%27s_diagonal_argument "Cantor's diagonal argument") or [Cantor's first uncountability proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor%27s_first_uncountability_proof "Cantor's first uncountability proof").

If |_A_| ≤ |_B_| and |_B_| ≤ |_A_|, then |_A_| = |_B_| (a fact known as [Schröder–Bernstein theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6der%E2%80%93Bernstein_theorem "Schröder–Bernstein theorem")). The [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice") is equivalent to the statement that |_A_| ≤ |_B_| or |_B_| ≤ |_A_| for every _A_, _B_.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-12)

## Cardinal numbers[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cardinality&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Cardinal numbers")]

Main article: [Cardinal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number")

In the above section, "cardinality" of a set was defined functionally. In other words, it was not defined as a specific object itself. However, such an object can be defined as follows.

The relation of having the same cardinality is called [equinumerosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equinumerosity "Equinumerosity"), and this is an [equivalence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation "Equivalence relation") on the [class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_(set_theory) "Class (set theory)") of all sets. The [equivalence class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") of a set _A_ under this relation, then, consists of all those sets which have the same cardinality as _A_. There are two ways to define the "cardinality of a set":

1. The cardinality of a set _A_ is defined as its equivalence class under equinumerosity.
2. A [representative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_(mathematics) "Representative (mathematics)") set is designated for each equivalence class. The most common choice is the [initial ordinal in that class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann_cardinal_assignment "Von Neumann cardinal assignment"). This is usually taken as the definition of [cardinal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number") in [axiomatic set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_set_theory "Axiomatic set theory").

Assuming the [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice"), the cardinalities of the [infinite sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_set "Infinite set") are denoted

## Finite, countable and uncountable sets[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cardinality&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Finite, countable and uncountable sets")]

If the [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice") holds, the [law of trichotomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichotomy_(mathematics) "Trichotomy (mathematics)") holds for cardinality. Thus we can make the following definitions:

- Any set _X_ with cardinality less than that of the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"), or | _X_ | < | **N** |, is said to be a [finite set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set").
- Any set _X_ that has the same cardinality as the set of the natural numbers, or | _X_ | = | **N** | = ℵ0![{\displaystyle \aleph _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/721cd7f8c15a2e72ad162bdfa5baea8eef98aab1), is said to be a [countably infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countably_infinite "Countably infinite") set.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-:1-10)
- Any set _X_ with cardinality greater than that of the natural numbers, or | _X_ | > | **N** |, for example | **R** | = �![{\displaystyle {\mathfrak {c}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b21924b960341255be18e538e51404718f29cbc0) > | **N** |, is said to be [uncountable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncountable_set "Uncountable set").

## Infinite sets[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cardinality&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Infinite sets")]

Our intuition gained from [finite sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set") breaks down when dealing with [infinite sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_set "Infinite set"). In the late 19th century [Georg Cantor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Cantor "Georg Cantor"), [Gottlob Frege](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottlob_Frege "Gottlob Frege"), [Richard Dedekind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Dedekind "Richard Dedekind") and others rejected the view that the whole cannot be the same size as the part.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality#cite_note-Cantor.1932-16)[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] One example of this is [Hilbert's paradox of the Grand Hotel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_paradox_of_the_Grand_Hotel "Hilbert's paradox of the Grand Hotel"). Indeed, Dedekind defined an infinite set as one that can be placed into a one-to-one correspondence with a strict subset (that is, having the same size in Cantor's sense); this notion of infinity is called [Dedekind infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dedekind_infinite "Dedekind infinite"). Cantor introduced the cardinal numbers, and showed—according to his bijection-based definition of size—that some infinite sets are greater than others. The smallest infinite cardinality is that of the natural numbers (ℵ0![{\displaystyle \aleph _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/721cd7f8c15a2e72ad162bdfa5baea8eef98aab1)).

### Cardinality of the continuum[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cardinality&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Cardinality of the continuum")]

(see [Cantor's diagonal argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor%27s_diagonal_argument "Cantor's diagonal argument") or [Cantor's first uncountability proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor%27s_first_uncountability_proof "Cantor's first uncountability proof")).

The [continuum hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_hypothesis "Continuum hypothesis") states that there is no [cardinal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number") between the cardinality of the reals and the cardinality of the natural numbers, that is,

2ℵ0=ℵ1![{\displaystyle 2^{\aleph _{0}}=\aleph _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac85246fab9f8d58c3f2a3d0662cc098e4b64a85)

However, this hypothesis can neither be proved nor disproved within the widely accepted [ZFC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZFC "ZFC") [axiomatic set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_set_theory "Axiomatic set theory"), if ZFC is consistent.

Cardinal arithmetic can be used to show not only that the number of points in a [real number line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number_line "Real number line") is equal to the number of points in any [segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") of that line, but that this is equal to the number of points on a plane and, indeed, in any finite-dimensional space. These results are highly counterintuitive, because they imply that there exist [proper subsets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_subset "Proper subset") and [proper supersets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_superset "Proper superset") of an infinite set _S_ that have the same size as _S_, although _S_ contains elements that do not belong to its subsets, and the supersets of _S_ contain elements that are not included in it.

The first of these results is apparent by considering, for instance, the [tangent function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_function "Tangent function"), which provides a [one-to-one correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-to-one_correspondence "One-to-one correspondence") between the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)") (−½π, ½π) and **R** (see also [Hilbert's paradox of the Grand Hotel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_paradox_of_the_Grand_Hotel "Hilbert's paradox of the Grand Hotel")).

The second result was first demonstrated by Cantor in 1878, but it became more apparent in 1890, when [Giuseppe Peano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppe_Peano "Giuseppe Peano") introduced the [space-filling curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_curve "Space-filling curve"), curved lines that twist and turn enough to fill the whole of any square, or cube, or [hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube"), or finite-dimensional space. These curves are not a direct proof that a line has the same number of points as a finite-dimensional space, but they can be used to obtain [such a proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_curve#Proof_that_a_square_and_its_side_contain_the_same_number_of_points "Space-filling curve").
